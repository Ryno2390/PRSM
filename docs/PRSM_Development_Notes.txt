However, this assumes the old plans for NWTN. However, I think we would benefit from a strategic pivot for NWTN.

This change was brought about by the realization that the latent GPU capacity available in consumer devices is staggering—it's essentially a "dark matter" of computing power that sits idle for 90% of the day.

Despite this, these consumer GPUs are almost useless when it comes to extreme compute resource constrains now facing frontier AI labs.

For a project like PRSM, this is a massive opportunity because we aren't competing for expensive H100 datacenter time; instead, we're harvesting "scraps" from millions of living rooms.

Here is the breakdown of the latent capacity we could potentially tap into as of 2026.

1. The "Big Three" Latent Sources

The most powerful pockets of idle GPU power reside in devices designed for high-performance bursts (gaming) that otherwise sit on standby. These include:
 
* Gaming PCs (Discrete GPUs): There are estimated to be over 300 million active gaming PCs globally with discrete GPUs (NVIDIA RTX or AMD Radeon). Even at a conservative 10% "uptime" for gaming, that leaves nearly 22 hours a day of idle power per machine.

* Next-Gen Consoles: As of early 2026, there are roughly 90 million PS5s and over 34 million Xbox Series X/S units in homes. These are essentially standardized, high-performance RDNA 2/3 GPUs that are completely idle when the TV is off.

* The "AI PC" Wave: A new category of laptops (MacBooks with M-series chips and Windows "AI PCs" with NPUs) is flooding the market. By the end of 2026, nearly 50% of new laptop shipments are expected to have dedicated AI acceleration hardware.

2. Capacity Estimates (The "Napkin Math")

If we managed to hook up even a tiny fraction of these devices to our proposed PRSM network, the collective TFLOPS (Teraflops) would dwarf any single supercomputer.

| Device Type | Average Latent Power (FP32) | Est. Global Idle Units | Potential Network Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| High-End Desktop | 30 - 80 TFLOPS | ~50 Million | 1.5 - 4.0 Zettaflops |
| Mid-Range Laptop | 5 - 15 TFLOPS | ~250 Million | 1.2 - 3.7 Zettaflops |
| Gaming Console | 10 - 12 TFLOPS | ~120 Million | 1.2 - 1.4 Zettaflops |
| Smartphone (High-end) | 1 - 3 TFLOPS | ~1.1 Billion | 1.1 - 3.3 Zettaflops |

Note: For context, the world's fastest supercomputers currently operate in the Exaflop range. A decentralized network tapping into 1% of this latent power could theoretically exceed the world's top 10 supercomputers combined.

3. The Challenges of "Hooking Them Up"

While the capacity is there, getting it onto the PRSM network involves three major hurdles:

* Thermal & Power Constraints: A smartphone might have 2 TFLOPS of power, but it can only run at that speed for 5 minutes before it gets too hot or dies. Our "batch settlement" logic would need to account for volatile nodes that disappear mid-job.

* The "Egress" Tax: Modern GPUs are fast, but home internet is slow. If a PRSM job requires 1GB of data but only 10 seconds of math, the internet speed becomes the bottleneck, not the GPU.

* User Friction: This is where “staking” comes in. Why would a gamer let PRSM nodes use their PC? We’d need to offer them a "yield" in our PRSM contract tokens that outweighs their electricity cost (the "Prosumer" model).

4. PRSM's Unique Advantage

Because PRSM is a network, we won’t need the "brute force" of a 4090 for every task.

* The Symbolic Layer: Can run on the "weak" Integrated GPUs or even the CPUs of older laptops.

* The Neural Layer: Can be routed specifically to the high-end discrete GPUs or the new NPUs in AI PCs.

By segmenting our "jobs" based on the hardware profile of the node, we could effectively turn a neighbor's idle PS5 into a "Reasoning Engine" for our network.

Clearly the main bottleneck we need to address to make PRSM work are network speeds. In a decentralized system, the bottleneck isn't usually the speed of the math; it's the Data-to-Compute Ratio. If it takes 2 seconds to download the data and 0.1 seconds to do the math, your network is 95% "waste."

Here are four clear strategies to break that bottleneck:

1. The "Code-to-Data" Shift (Mobile Agents)

Instead of sending massive datasets to our agents, we send the Agents to the data.

* The Traditional Way: Download a 1GB database to a local PC -> Run a query -> Send results.
* The PRSM Way: Send a tiny 50KB "Agent" (the logic) to a node that already has the data (like a local database or a cached file). The agent runs locally and only sends back the final 1KB "Conclusion.”
* Why it works: we are moving the smallest possible unit (the logic) across the wire instead of the largest (the data).

2. Hierarchical "Regional" Aggregation
This mimics how the physical mail system or the internet backbone works. We group our nodes into local clusters.

* The Mesh: Nodes in the same city or on the same high-speed ISP subnet perform "Micro-Settlements" among themselves.
* The Hub: One "Staked" node acts as a regional aggregator. It collects 100 small batches and compresses them into a single "Regional Proof."
* The Result: Only the compressed proof ever travels across the "slow" long-distance internet to hit our Ethereum settlement layer.

3. Model Distillation & Quantization
Since we’re leveraging latent GPUs (like those in a phone or a PS5), we can’t send them 80GB model files.

* Quantization: Shrink the weights of our neural components from 32-bit to 4-bit or even 1.5-bit. This reduces the "download" time by 80% with minimal loss in reasoning.
* Pruning: Use the symbolic layer to "filter" what the neural layer needs to see. If the symbolic logic says "This data is irrelevant," the GPU never even has to download that chunk of the dataset.

4. Edge Caching (The "Netflix" Model)
If our PRSM network frequently runs similar jobs, we can use a "Content Delivery Network" (CDN) approach for AI weights and data.

* The Strategy: Popular "Knowledge Bases" or "Model Weights" are pre-staged on idle nodes during off-peak hours (like 3 AM).
* The Benefit: When a job starts at 2 PM, the node already has 90% of what it needs locally. The "internet bottleneck" is bypassed because the data transfer happened hours ago.

By combining BitTorrent-style sharding with Mobile Agents we are essentially creating the "Holy Grail" of decentralized architecture. It transforms the network from a slow file-sharing service into a high-speed, distributed brain.

In this model, we aren't just moving files; we are creating a Spatial Computing Fabric:

1. The "Swarm-Aware" Agent

In a standard BitTorrent setup, a file is broken into 1MB "pieces." In PRSM, a Mobile Agent doesn't just look for a file; it looks for the Swarm.

* The Logic: Instead of an agent waiting for a 1GB dataset to download, the agent "hops" to a node that already has the specific 1MB shard it needs. The agent executes its tiny piece of logic on that 1MB shard locally and then moves (or clones itself) to the next node in the swarm that has the next shard.
* Result: You never move the 1GB data. You move a 50KB agent across a high-speed local "swarm" of peers.

2. Distributed Vector Sharding

PRSM will leverage "embeddings" (mathematical representations of data), to shard data based on meaning rather than just file size.

* The Logic: we shard data so that "similar" information lives on the same group of nodes (a "neighborhood"). When a Mobile Agent needs to answer a specific question, it doesn't query the whole network. It uses the Torrent DHT (Distributed Hash Table) to find the "neighborhood" that holds relevant data and only visits those nodes.
* Result: This drastically reduces "Network Hops," making the agent's journey much faster.

3. Parallel "Map-Reduce" via Shards

This is where Staking and Batch Settlement become critical.

* The Logic: we can broadcast a single "Task" to the entire swarm. Each node holding a shard runs a local "Mobile Agent" instance. These agents all work in parallel on their tiny 1MB pieces.
* The Settle: They send their "Mini-Results" to a staked Aggregator Node. This node combines the results and submits the final batch settlement to Ethereum.
* Result: You’ve effectively turned a 1,000-hour job into a 1-hour job by utilizing the latent GPU power of the entire swarm simultaneously.

How to Prototype This

1. Shard our dataset: Use a library like libtorrent to create a private swarm for our "contract" data.
2. Define the Agent: Create a lightweight Python or WASM script that performs a simple calculation (e.g., "Count the instances of X in this shard").
3. The "Jump" Logic: Give the agent a list of "Peer IPs" from the torrent tracker. Have it ssh or use a POST request to "carry" itself to the next node after finishing a shard.

By combining these, we’re not just building a network; we’re building a Distributed Operating System where the data is the "hard drive" and the mobile agents are the "CPU threads."

The workflow isn't just "cloud computing" - it is more like a biological immune system reacting to a new stimulus.

Here is the step-by-step lifecycle of real world example, from a researcher’s desk to an idle PS5 and back.

1. The Trigger: LLM + MCP

The researcher who has set up a PRSM node types a complex query into Gemini 3.1 Pro: "Analyze the NADA vehicle registration trends in North Carolina for 2025 vs 2026, looking specifically for EV adoption shifts."

* The MCP Bridge: Gemini 3.1 Pro doesn't have the 50GB NADA dataset in its "brain." It sees the PRSM Model Context Protocol (MCP) tool and realizes: "I don't have this data, but the PRSM network does.”
* The Submission: Gemini sends the "intent" (the logic needed for the analysis) to the PRSM gateway. This is converted into a Mobile Agent—a tiny packet of code (likely WASM) that contains the instructions but none of the data.

2. Distributed Vector Sharding: Finding the "Neighborhood"

The NADA dataset isn't one giant file; it was sharded when it was uploaded to PRSM.

* Semantic Sharding: Instead of random splitting, PRSM uses Distributed Vector Sharding. Data about "North Carolina," "EVs," and "2025/2026 Registrations" was grouped together onto specific nodes based on its mathematical meaning (embeddings).
* The Lookup: The PRSM network looks at the researcher's prompt and identifies the specific "shards" needed. It finds that shard #NC-EV-2025 happens to be stored on that idle PlayStation 5 in Apex, NC.

3. The Swarm Agent: Moving the Logic, Not the Data

Now, the Mobile Agent get to work.

* The Hop: Instead of the PS5 uploading 5GB of registration data to the researcher (which would take an hour), the 50KB Mobile Agent "hops" to the PS5.
* The Swarm: Simultaneously, other shards of the data (e.g., national averages for comparison) are sitting on other idle devices. The network creates a "Swarm" of these agents, all heading to different "neighborhoods" (nodes) at once.

4. Parallel "Map-Reduce": The PS5 Wakes Up

The PS5 is sitting in "Rest Mode." The PRSM daemon (running in a low-power container or the console's background media layer) receives the Mobile Agent.

* The Map (Local Compute): The PS5 uses its RDNA 2 GPU (capable of ~10 TFLOPS) to run the analysis locally on the shard it already has. It filters the data, calculates the EV growth percentages for its specific shard, and produces a tiny "summary" (e.g., a 10KB JSON file).  
* The Reduce (Aggregation): The PS5 doesn't talk back to Gemini yet. It sends its 10KB summary to a Staked Aggregator Node. This node collects the "summaries" from all the PS5s, Xboxes, and PCs in the swarm.

5. Settlement & Reward (FTNS)

Once the Aggregator Node has the final answer, it submits a Batch Settlement to the Ethereum basenet to prove the work was done.

* The Proof: The PS5 provides a cryptographic "Proof of Execution" showing it ran the exact code requested on the exact data shard it holds.
* The Reward: The PRSM smart contract automatically releases a fragment of FTNS (tokens) to the PS5 owner's wallet.
* The Answer: Gemini 3.1 Pro receives the final, synthesized report from the PRSM network and presents it to the researcher: "EV adoption in NC grew by 14% in 2025..."

By the time the individual gets home from work, their PS5 has "mined" enough FTNS to pay for a month of PlayStation Plus, and the researcher has a high-tier analysis that would have cost $500 in traditional cloud credits—all because the Mobile Agent did the "commuting" instead of the data.

All of this is possible because of WASM (WebAssembly) - essentially the "shipping container" for the mobile agents.

If our PRSM network is a digital highway, WASM is the standardized vehicle that allows code to travel from a researcher’s high-end PC to a random PlayStation 5 without crashing, leaking data, or needing to be rewritten.

1. It’s a "Universal Translator"

Normally, code written for a Mac won’t run on an Xbox, and code for a Windows PC won’t run on a smartphone.

* The WASM way: we write our agent in a language like Rust, C++, or Go, and compile it into a .wasm file.
* The Result: That one file can now run on any device that has a "WASM Runtime" (which almost every modern device, including consoles and browsers, now have).

2. It’s a "Security Sandbox"

This is the most critical part for PRSM. You are asking a stranger to run your code on their PS5.

* The Risk: Without WASM, a malicious agent could steal the user's saved games, passwords, or brick the console.
* The WASM Solution: WASM runs in a strict "sandbox." It has no access to the local hard drive, camera, or network unless the owner explicitly grants permission. It can "do the math" inside its little box, but it can't peek outside of it.

3. It’s "Near-Native" Speed

Unlike JavaScript (which is interpreted line-by-line and can be slow), WASM is a binary format.

* It is pre-optimized so that when it arrives at the PS5, the hardware can execute it at nearly the same speed as a game or a native app. This is why it’s perfect for the "Map-Reduce" jobs I described—it’s fast enough to handle heavy data analysis.

4. WASI: The "System Interface"
While WASM started in the browser, a newer standard called WASI (WebAssembly System Interface) allows it to run on servers and devices directly.

* This is what our PRSM "daemon" on the PS5 would use. It provides the agent with a tiny "virtual" window to see only the specific data shard it needs to analyze, while keeping the rest of the system invisible.

In our concrete example, when an LLM creates that "Mobile Agent," it is actually generating a WASM module. That module is small enough to be sent over a standard internet connection in a blink, secure enough for the PS5 owner to feel safe, and fast enough to finish the vehicle registration analysis before the individual gets home from work.

What’s great is that this architecture is fast.  A mature PRSM system wouldn't just be "competitive" with a frontier API—for specific types of data-heavy reasoning, it could be substantially faster.

The speed of a centralized API (like Gemini or OpenAI) is currently limited by the "speed of light" (the distance between you and the data center) and "congestion" (how many people are hitting that same server). PRSM changes the math by moving the compute to the edge.

Here is how the speeds would compare in a mature 2026 landscape:

1. The Latency Gap (Round-Trip Time)

* Centralized API: Even with a high-speed connection, your request typically travels hundreds of miles to a data center. Average round-trip latency for a frontier model in 2026 hovers around 40-60ms just for the data to travel, before any "thinking" happens.  
* PRSM Edge: If the "Mobile Agent" finds the data on a local node (like your neighbor's PS5 or an edge server at a nearby cell tower), that latency drops to 10-15ms.

2. The "Throughput" Advantage (Parallelism)

This is where PRSM leaves APIs in the dust.

* Centralized API: If you ask an API to analyze a 100GB dataset, that server has to process it sequentially or within a single cluster. You are essentially waiting in a single-file line.
* PRSM Swarm: Because PRSM shards the data, a 100GB job is split among 10,000 nodes simultaneously. This is the difference between one chef cooking a 100-course meal and 100 chefs each cooking one dish. For massive analytical tasks, PRSM could finish in seconds what an API might take minutes to "stream" back to you.

For a simple "Hello, how are you?", the API will always win on convenience. But for the "PRSM use case"—analyzing massive, decentralized data like the NADA registration set—PRSM would be orders of magnitude faster because it eliminates the need to move the data.

In a mature state, the researcher wouldn't even notice the "network" exists; they would just see the final report appear almost instantly after hitting enter.

Leveraging an LLM to generate the Mobile Agents would turn our network from a passive file-sharer into a "Smart Swarm."

1. The "WASM Architect" Fine-Tune
We could create a version of an open weight LLM fine-tuned on WebAssembly (WASM) binary patterns and device-specific hardware specs.

* The Task: Instead of writing a generic Python script, the model looks at the target node (the PS5) and says: "This device has 16GB of GDDR6 and an RDNA 2 GPU. I will compile the Mobile Agent with specific RDNA-optimized kernels for the NADA analysis."
* The Benefit: The agent isn't just "running" on the PS5; it’s being custom-built for that specific console's silicon in real-time.

2. The "Prompt-to-Agent" Pipeline
Using an open weight "Frontier" model means it can handle the complex reasoning required to bridge the two layers of PRSM: 

* The Reasoning: It takes the researcher’s prompt and decides: "The Symbolic Layer needs to handle the year-over-year filtering, but the Neural Layer needs to handle the 'EV Sentiment' analysis of the vehicle descriptions.”
* The Output: It doesn't just output text; it outputs a Manifest—a JSON-structured set of instructions that triggers the creation of the WASM Mobile Agent.

3. Hardware-Aware Code Generation

Because frontier, open weight models have large context windows, they can "read" the entire technical documentation of the target hardware (like the PS5’s developer API) while it writes the agent.

* The "Device-Specific" Instruction: The model can insert specific WASI (System Interface) calls into the agent to ensure it only touches the "NADA Shard" and stays within the PS5's thermal limits so it doesn't kick the user out of "Rest Mode."

If we integrated fine tuned "Agent Maker" into our prototype, the flow would look like this:

1. Input: Researcher prompt via MCP.
2. The LLM: Analyzes the prompt + target hardware profiles (from the Staking registry).
3. The Forge: The fine tuned LLM generates the WASM Agent code optimized for those profiles.
4. The Swarm: The agents are dispatched to the PS5s and PCs.
5. The Settlement: The agents report back, and the batch is settled on Ethereum.

By positioning the LLM as the "Reasoning Engine" (the brain) and the edge devices as the "Compute Fabric" (the muscle), we’re creating a system that mirrors how a biological organism works: the brain doesn't personally move the arm; it sends a tiny electrical signal (the Mobile Agent) that tells the muscle how to move.

Here is how our vision segments into a clear, actionable strategy:

1. The "Open Source Model" Strategy
Instead of just a general-purpose chatbot, the PRSM-optimized model would be a specialized "Architect Model."

* The Fine-Tune: we would optimize it not for writing poetry, but for Agentic Decomposition.
* The Skill: It looks at a user’s prompt and its primary output is a WASM manifest. It understands the rules of our network and ensures the neural jobs it assigns to the edge are as small and efficient as possible.
* The Advantage: Because it’s open-source, any node can run it locally or call it via a PRSM-hosted cloud endpoint if they don't have the hardware.

2. The Choice: Local vs. Cloud

This is a brilliant way to handle the Network Onboarding:

* Low-Power Nodes: A smartphone might use a Cloud-based "Reasoning Engine" (via our MCP tools) to decide what to do with its local data shard.
* Power-User Nodes: A high-end Mac or PC runs our proprietary, fine-tuned model locally. This is the "Zero-Latency" mode—the reasoning and the compute happen on the same machine, offering maximum privacy and speed.
* The Market: the FTNS token becomes the "currency" of this choice. Using a Cloud reasoning engine might cost a tiny fraction of a token, while running it locally is "free" but uses your own electricity.

3. The "MCP Everywhere" Layer

By making PRSM’s tools available to any model (Gemini, Claude, or our own), we avoid the "platform lock-in" trap.

* Standardization: PRSM acts as the Operating System. Whether a researcher uses Gemini 3.1 Pro or Claude Opus 4.6, the "tools" they use to talk to the PS5s and PCs remain the same.
* Interoperability: This allows our network to benefit from the massive R&D budgets of Google and Anthropic while keeping our specialized, fine-tuned, proprietary model as the "efficient default" for the network's internal operations.

I envision that this fine tuned LLM will be the newly conceived NWTN. What I envision is that nodes on PRSM have a choice - either local or cloud models. PRSM will make MCP tools and skills available to all models. PRSM will develop its own open source model (NWTN) that is specifically optimized for the architecture we’ve developed so far. The LLMs will be the reasoning component and the heavy lifting in terms of raw computer power will be the edge devices - a conceptual shift from Cloud-First AI to Sovereign-Edge AI.

I haven’t figured out the price architecture yet. I don’t know if users will set their own prices in a true market or if we go with a menu approach with set prices for certain functions. Likely it will be some sort of hybrid: for example, set prices for compute based on size but user determined pricing for proprietary data sets. A hybrid pricing model is likely our strongest move because it balances predictability for the compute providers (the "muscle") with value-based discovery for the data owners (the "intellectual property"). We’ve seen decentralized networks like Render and Akash move toward "commodity" pricing for compute, while data marketplaces like Ocean Protocol have proven that fixed-rate data pricing often fails because data value is contextual.

Here is a breakdown of how our hybrid "Menu + Market" architecture could look in practice:

1. The "Menu" (Standardized Compute)

For the raw "muscle" (the PS5s, PCs, and MacBooks), we want a Deterministic Pricing Menu. This makes it easy for a researcher to estimate their costs before they hit "Run."
* The Metric: We could use TFLOPS-Hours (floating-point operations) or a simpler PRSM-Unit that combines GPU power, RAM, and bandwidth.
* The Tiers:
    * Tier 1 (IoT/Mobile): Cheap, slow, but highly distributed. Good for simple symbolic filtering.
    * Tier 2 (Consumer Console/PC): The "Workhorse" tier (your PS5 example). Mid-range pricing.
    * Tier 3 (H100/Enterprise Edge): Premium pricing for high-speed neural inference.
* Why a Menu? Compute providers want to know: "If I leave my PS5 on for 8 hours, how much FTNS am I guaranteed to make?" A menu provides that stability.

2. The "Market" (Proprietary Data Sets)

Data value is not linear. 1GB of cat videos is worth $0, but 1GB of NADA vehicle registration data is worth thousands. For this, we need Dynamic Market Pricing.
* The Mechanism: Data owners (like NADA) set a "Base Access Fee" plus a "Success Royalty."
* Bidding: If multiple researchers want the same data, the price can stay stable, but the data owner can also choose to "Auction" exclusive early access to new shards (e.g., the first look at 2026 Q1 data).
* The Staking Connection: To prevent "data scraping," we could require researchers to stake a certain amount of FTNS to access high-value datasets. If they attempt to leak the data, their stake is slashed.

3. The "Broker" (PRSM-Core Reasoning)

Since we’re building out own model (NWTN) to handle the reasoning, this acts as the "Pricing Orchestrator."
* The Quote: When a researcher submits a prompt, NWTN analyzes the task and gives a "Quote": "To answer this, I need 500 T2 Compute Units ($5.00) and access to the NADA '26 Shard ($25.00). Total: $30.00 FTNS."
* The Arbitrage: If the "Menu" price for compute is too high and no one is taking the jobs, the network can automatically trigger a "Spot Market" (similar to AWS Spot Instances) to lower prices and attract idle hardware.

Real-World Example

In our NADA scenario:
1. NADA lists their 2026 NC dataset at 5 FTNS per query.
2. The PS5 Owner sees a "Work Order" on the menu for 0.1 FTNS per 1MB shard analyzed.
3. The Researcher sees a total cost. They pay the FTNS; the protocol splits the payment: 80% to NADA, 15% to the PS5 swarm, and 5% to the PRSM treasury for maintaining the "Reasoning" model.

This approach prevents the "race to the bottom" for high-value data while ensuring the "muscle" (the hardware) stays competitive with centralized clouds like AWS.

This re-conception views PRSM as the connective tissue, not the muscle fibers themselves. By focusing on the "Infrastructure Wrapper," we effectively turn the frontier labs from potential competitors into upstream suppliers. If OpenAI or Google releases a "super-model" tomorrow, PRSM doesn't lose; it simply gains a more powerful engine to plug into its existing pipes. This positioning—focusing on Utility, Orchestration, and Settlement—is far more defensible than trying to out-spend the giants on raw training.

Frontier labs are in a "Red Queen's Race" where they must run faster just to stay in place. PRSM, however, sits at the Interface Layer.
* The Problem with Frontier Models: They are "Brains in a Vat." They are incredibly smart but have zero "Hands" to touch real-world data securely or "Wallets" to pay for their own compute.
* The PRSM Solution: You provide the WASM Hands (Mobile Agents) and the FTNS Wallet (Ethereum Settlement). You make the "Brain" actually useful for a researcher who needs to touch 50GB of NADA data without moving it to the cloud.
